## 1. Chromosomes: Structure and Function

A chromosome is a thread-like structure made of **DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid)** and associated proteins (primarily histones). During cell division, chromatin condenses into the visible chromosome structure.

### Key Structural Features
* **Centromere:** The constriction point where sister chromatids are joined. It is the attachment site for spindle fibers during cell division.
* **Telomeres:** Repetitive DNA sequences at the ends of chromosomes that protect them (like the plastic tip of a shoelace). They shorten with each cell division (linked to ageing — the Hayflick Limit).
* **Chromatids:** After DNA replication, each chromosome consists of two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere.

### Human Chromosome Complement (Karyotype)
| Type | Number | Role |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Autosomes** | 22 pairs (44 chromosomes) | Carry genes governing all bodily traits except sex determination. |
| **Sex Chromosomes (Allosomes)** | 1 pair | Determine biological sex. Female = XX, Male = XY. |
| **Total** | **46 chromosomes (23 pairs)** | Normal human somatic cell count. |

## 2. The Biological Basis of Life: DNA

### A. DNA Structure (Watson-Crick Model, 1953)
DNA is a double helix — two anti-parallel strands of nucleotides wound around each other like a twisted ladder.
* **The Backbone (Sugar-Phosphate):** Each strand consists of alternating deoxyribose sugar and phosphate groups.
* **The Rungs (Nitrogenous Bases — Chargaff's Rules):** The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs:
  * **Adenine (A) ↔ Thymine (T)** (2 hydrogen bonds)
  * **Guanine (G) ↔ Cytosine (C)** (3 hydrogen bonds)

### B. DNA Replication (Semi-Conservative Model)
DNA replication is the process by which a cell makes an identical copy of its DNA before cell division. It occurs during the **S-phase** of the cell cycle.

```mermaid
graph TD
    A[Helicase unwinds the double helix<br>creating a Replication Fork] --> B[Primase synthesizes short RNA primers<br>to mark the start site]
    B --> C[DNA Polymerase III adds nucleotides<br>5' to 3' direction on each template strand]
    C --> D[Leading Strand: synthesized continuously<br>Lagging Strand: synthesized as Okazaki fragments]
    D --> E[DNA Polymerase I removes RNA primers<br>and fills gaps]
    E --> F[DNA Ligase joins all fragments<br>into one continuous strand]
    F --> G[Result: 2 identical daughter DNA molecules<br>each with one original + one new strand]
```

> [!TIP]
> **Mnemonic for DNA Replication Enzymes:** **H P P L L** (Helicase, Primase, Polymerase III, Polymerase I, Ligase) — "**H**eartily **P**lease **P**rofessors, **L**earn **L**aboratory."

### C. The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology (Francis Crick, 1958)
Information flows in one direction: **DNA → RNA → Protein**. This one-way flow is the foundational principle of molecular biology.

```mermaid
graph LR
    A[DNA] -->|Transcription<br>RNA Polymerase| B[mRNA]
    B -->|Translation<br>Ribosome + tRNA| C[Protein / Polypeptide]
    A -.->|Replication<br>DNA Polymerase| A
```

1. **Transcription (in nucleus):** A segment of DNA (a gene) is used as a template to synthesize a complementary messenger RNA (mRNA) strand by RNA Polymerase. The RNA uses **Uracil (U)** instead of Thymine.
2. **Translation (in cytoplasm/ribosome):** The mRNA sequence is read in triplets (codons) at the ribosome. Each codon specifies one amino acid. Transfer RNA (tRNA) molecules bring the correct amino acid to the growing polypeptide chain until a stop codon is reached, releasing the complete protein.

## 3. Gene Mutations vs. Chromosomal Aberrations

| Feature | Gene Mutation | Chromosomal Aberration |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level** | Nucleotide level (single base pair) | Chromosome level (large-scale structural or numerical) |
| **Scale** | Micro (invisible under microscope) | Macro (visible under microscope via karyotyping) |
| **Cause** | Errors in DNA replication, mutagens (radiation, chemicals) | Errors in meiosis (non-disjunction), chromosome breakage |
| **Example** | Sickle Cell Anaemia (single nucleotide change in HBB gene) | Down Syndrome (extra chromosome 21) |

## 4. Chromosomal Aberrations: Numerical Aberrations

Numerical aberrations arise from **non-disjunction** — the failure of chromosomes to separate correctly during meiosis I or meiosis II.

```mermaid
graph TD
    A[Non-disjunction during Meiosis] --> B[Aneuploidy: Abnormal chromosome count]
    B --> C[Monosomy: 2n - 1 = 45 chromosomes]
    B --> D[Trisomy: 2n + 1 = 47 chromosomes]
    A --> E[Polyploidy: Extra complete sets of chromosomes<br>Rare in humans, common in plants]
```

### Major Chromosomal Syndromes: A Comparison Table

| Syndrome | Karyotype | Type | Key Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Down Syndrome** | 47, +21 (Trisomy 21) | Autosomal Trisomy | Intellectual disability, flat facial profile, epicanthic folds, congenital heart defects. Risk sharply increases with advanced maternal age (>35 years). |
| **Patau Syndrome** | 47, +13 (Trisomy 13) | Autosomal Trisomy | Severe intellectual disability, polydactyly, cleft palate, congenital heart defects. Usually fatal within first year. |
| **Edward Syndrome** | 47, +18 (Trisomy 18) | Autosomal Trisomy | Severe intellectual disability, overlapping fingers, heart defects. 90% die within first year. |
| **Turner Syndrome** | 45, XO | Sex Chromosome Monosomy | Phenotypically female. Short stature, webbed neck, widely spaced nipples, underdeveloped ovaries, **absolute sterility**. |
| **Klinefelter Syndrome** | 47, XXY | Sex Chromosome Trisomy | Phenotypically male. Tall, small testes, gynecomastia (breast development), reduced masculinity, **infertility**. |
| **Super Female (Triple X)** | 47, XXX | Sex Chromosome Trisomy | Phenotypically normal female. Usually fertile. May have slightly lower IQ. Often undetected. |
| **XYY Syndrome (Jacob's)** | 47, XYY | Sex Chromosome Trisomy | Phenotypically normal male. Tall stature, mild behavioral problems. |

> [!TIP]
> **Mnemonic for Autosomal Trisomies:** **D P E** (21, 13, 18) → **"Down Patau Edward"** — D is lowest (21), P is next (13), E is highest (18). Or simply: **D-P-E = Down-Patau-Edwards**.

## 5. Chromosomal Aberrations: Structural Aberrations

Structural changes occur when chromosome breaks do not rejoin correctly, generating rearrangements.

```mermaid
graph LR
    A[Chromosome Breakage] --> B[Deletion: Segment Lost]
    A --> C[Duplication: Segment repeated]
    A --> D[Inversion: Segment flips 180° and reattaches]
    A --> E[Translocation: Segment moves to non-homologous chromosome]
```

| Type | Mechanism | Effect / Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Deletion** | Loss of a chromosomal segment. | *Cri-du-chat Syndrome* (deletion of short arm of Chr 5): Cat-like cry in infants, intellectual disability. |
| **Duplication** | Extra copy of a chromosomal segment present. | Gene dosage imbalance; can cause developmental abnormalities. |
| **Inversion** | A segment breaks out, rotates 180°, and is reinserted. | Gene expression may be disrupted if the break occurs within a gene. |
| **Translocation** | A segment from one chromosome is transferred to a non-homologous chromosome. | *Robertsonian Translocation:* Chr 21 fuses to Chr 14, causing **Familial Down Syndrome** (occurring even in younger mothers; hereditary risk). |

## 6. Gene Mutations: Point Mutations

While chromosomal aberrations are large-scale, point mutations (gene mutations) affect individual nucleotides.

| Type | Mechanism | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Substitution** | One base is replaced by another. | *Sickle Cell Anaemia:* A→T substitution in codon 6 of the HBB gene changes Glutamic Acid to Valine, distorting the red blood cell into a sickle shape. |
| **Insertion** | An extra base is added. | Causes a **frameshift mutation** — the entire reading frame of codons shifts, producing a completely different and usually non-functional protein. |
| **Deletion** | A base is removed. | Also causes a **frameshift mutation**. |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Sickle Cell Anaemia & Malaria (UPSC Gold Standard):** The sickle cell allele is found at high frequency in malaria-endemic regions (Sub-Saharan Africa, parts of India). Heterozygotes (carriers) have *increased resistance* to malaria — a classic example of **Heterozygote Advantage / Balancing Selection**. This is why the harmful allele is maintained in the population by natural selection.

## 7. Mutagenic Agents (Mutagens)

Mutations are caused by physical, chemical, or biological agents:
1. **Physical Mutagens:**
   * **Ionizing Radiation (X-rays, Gamma rays):** Cause chromosome breakage and base oxidation. First demonstrated by H.J. Muller (1927) in fruit flies (Nobel Prize, 1946).
   * **UV Radiation:** Causes thymine-thymine dimers, blocking DNA replication. Normally repaired by Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER). Failure leads to Xeroderma Pigmentosum.
2. **Chemical Mutagens:**
   * **Base Analogues** (5-Bromouracil): Mimic DNA bases and are incorporated into the strand, causing substitutions.
   * **Alkylating Agents** (Mustard Gas): Add alkyl groups to bases, causing mispairing.
   * **Intercalating Agents** (Acridine Dyes): Insert between base pairs, causing insertions/deletions.
3. **Biological Mutagens:**
   * **Viruses:** Can integrate their DNA into the host genome, disrupting gene function (e.g., HPV causing cervical cancer).

## 8. Methods in Human Genetics (Unit 9.1)

Since controlled breeding experiments are impossible in humans, geneticists use indirect laboratory methods to detect, analyze, and treat genetic conditions:

### A. Biochemical Methods (Inborn Errors & Polymorphism)
Biochemical methods analyze proteins, enzymes, and other metabolic compounds to identify genetic polymorphisms and **Inborn Errors of Metabolism** (inherited metabolic disorders caused by single-gene defects).
* **Core Techniques:**
  * *Chromatography (Paper/Gas/HPLC):* Used to separate and identify amino acids or organic acids in body fluids (e.g., detecting elevated phenylalanine in urine).
  * *Electrophoresis (Gel Electrophoresis):* Separates serum proteins and hemoglobin variants based on their size and electrical charge under an electric field. Crucial for mapping haptoglobin, transferrin, and hemoglobin polymorphisms (e.g., separating $HbA$ from $HbS$ in sickle-cell diagnosis).
  * *Spectrophotometry:* Measures the light absorption of biochemical solutions to determine enzyme activity levels.
* **Diseases Diagnosed:**
  * **Phenylketonuria (PKU):** Deficiency of phenylalanine hydroxylase, leading to mental retardation if untreated.
  * **Alkaptonuria:** Deficiency of homogentisic acid oxidase, causing black urine and arthritis.
  * **Albinism:** Inability to synthesize melanin from tyrosine.

### B. Immunological Methods (Serogenetics & HLA Typing)
Immunological methods utilize the highly specific **antigen-antibody reactions** to map genetic variations on cell surfaces and analyze tissue compatibility.
* **Core Techniques:**
  * *Hemagglutination (Blood Grouping):* Mixing red blood cells with specific antibodies (antisera) to observe clumping. This is the gold standard for mapping **ABO** and **Rh** blood group systems.
  * *ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay):* Uses enzyme-labeled antibodies to detect and quantify specific proteins or antibodies in blood serum.
  * *HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) Typing:* Analyzes the highly polymorphic major histocompatibility complex (MHC) proteins on white blood cells. 
* **Key Applications:**
  * *Disputed Paternity:* Comparing HLA haplotypes or blood group antigens of the mother, child, and alleged father to exclude or prove paternity.
  * *Organ Transplantation:* Matching HLA profiles between donors and recipients to prevent immune-mediated graft rejection.
  * **Value Addition (UPSC Mains):** **CAR-T Cell Therapy** represents the convergence of immunology, genetics, and biotechnology, where a patient's own T-cells are genetically modified (often using CRISPR) to target and destroy cancer cells. Furthermore, **ELISA** and advanced immuno-electrophoresis are now utilized extensively in forensic anthropology for individual identification.

### C. Recombinant DNA and DNA Technologies (Molecular Tools)
Recombinant DNA and molecular technologies directly analyze and manipulate the **DNA sequence** itself, representing the modern cutting-edge of physical anthropology and genomic medicine.
* **Core Techniques:**
  * *PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction):* Invented by **Kary Mullis** in 1983. A molecular photocopy machine that amplifies a tiny, specific segment of DNA millions of times, allowing analysis of minute samples (e.g., fossil bones, hair roots at a crime scene).
  * *RFLP (Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphism):* Uses restriction enzymes to cut DNA at specific sequence sites. Variations in the resulting fragment lengths (due to mutations altering the cut sites) are separated by gel electrophoresis to map mutations.
  * *Southern Blotting:* Transfers electrophoretically separated DNA fragments to a membrane, followed by hybridization with labeled DNA probes to detect specific genes.
  * *DNA Sequencing (Sanger / Next-Gen Sequencing):* Deciphers the exact, letter-by-letter nucleotide sequence of a gene or the entire genome.
* **Key Applications:**
  * **DNA Profiling / Fingerprinting:** Analyzes hypervariable Short Tandem Repeats (STRs) in non-coding DNA to create unique genetic profiles for forensic identification and paternity testing.
  * **Gene Mapping & Genome Studies:** Mapping the exact physical chromosomal location of genes (e.g., the Human Genome Project).
  * **Gene Therapy:** Replaced a defective, disease-causing gene with a functional, recombinant copy using viral vectors (e.g., treating severe combined immunodeficiency - SCID).
  * **Value Addition (UPSC Mains) - CRISPR-Cas9 & Recent Variants:** The use of CRISPR as "molecular scissors" has revolutionized anthropology and medicine. Recent variants like **Base Editing and Prime Editing** allow single-letter DNA changes without breaking the strand.
  * **Value Addition - Indian Context:** India's **"Sickle Cell-Free India by 2047"** mission leverages modern DNA technology to address tribal health issues. The development of **BIRSA 101** (an indigenous CRISPR-based therapy for Sickle Cell Disease) and **TnpB-based editing** provides low-cost, IP-free alternatives for genomic medicine in developing nations.

#### PYQ 1: "Write a short note on protein synthesis." [2019, 10 Marks]
* **Introduction:** Protein synthesis is the biological process by which cells generate specific proteins dictated by DNA. It is the physical manifestation of the Central Dogma of Molecular Biology (DNA → RNA → Protein).
* **Body:**
  * **Phase 1: Transcription (In the Nucleus):** 
    * The DNA double helix unwinds at a specific gene locus.
    * *RNA Polymerase* reads the template DNA strand and synthesizes a complementary, single-stranded messenger RNA (mRNA). In RNA, Uracil (U) replaces Thymine (T).
    * The mRNA undergoes splicing (removing non-coding introns) and exits the nucleus via nuclear pores.
  * **Phase 2: Translation (In the Cytoplasm/Ribosome):**
    * The mRNA binds to a ribosome (the cellular protein factory).
    * The mRNA is read in sets of three nucleotides called *codons*. Each codon specifies one amino acid.
    * Transfer RNA (tRNA) molecules bring the corresponding amino acids to the ribosome. Their *anticodons* pair with the mRNA codons.
    * The ribosome links the amino acids together with peptide bonds, forming a polypeptide chain.
* **Conclusion:** The chain folds into a functional 3D protein structure. This process explains how a genotype (DNA sequence) is translated into a phenotype (physical trait) in human biology.
