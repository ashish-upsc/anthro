## 1. Concept of Human Growth and Development

Anthropologists study growth to understand human variation across socio-cultural and geographic dimensions, as adaptive responses to environmental conditions.

### Growth vs. Development
| **Growth** | **Development** |
| :--- | :--- |
| Quantitative change in physical characteristics (size, height, weight). | Qualitative/overall change in form and structure for enhanced functional skills. |
| Measured directly (inches, cm, kg). | Difficult to measure directly (requires functional assessments). |
| Stops when maturity is attained. | Progressive and continuous throughout life. |
| Result of cellular processes: Hyperplasia (cell number), Hypertrophy (cell size), and Accretion (intercellular substances). | Result of differentiation (pluripotent cells becoming specialized) and behavioural learning. |

### Principles of Growth and Development
1. **Cephalocaudal Development:** Proceeds from head to toe. (e.g., infants control head/neck before lower limbs).
2. **Proximodistal Development:** Proceeds from the center of the body to the periphery. (e.g., control of trunk before fingers).
3. **General to Specific:** Movements go from generalized/reflexive to highly specific and refined (e.g., whole hand grasp to thumb/forefinger pincer grasp).
4. **Different Rates:** Different body parts and cognitive functions develop at varying rates (e.g., neural tissue grows fastest early on).

## 2. Key Growth Phenomena (UPSC Value Addition)

### A. Catch-Up Growth
When a child experiences a period of illness or severe malnutrition, their growth rate drops significantly. Once the constraint is removed (e.g., nutrition is restored or the disease is cured), the child's growth rate dramatically accelerates beyond the normal rate for their age to "catch up" to their genetically predetermined growth curve. This demonstrates the strong genetic canalization of human growth.

### B. Secular Trend in Growth
A "secular trend" refers to a long-term change in a measurable trait across successive generations. Over the past 150 years in developed and developing nations, there has been a positive secular trend where each generation is taller, heavier, and reaches puberty earlier (earlier age of menarche) than the previous one. This is attributed to better nutrition, improved sanitation, reduction in childhood infectious diseases, and better medical care.

## 3. Patterns of Human Growth (Scammon's Curves)

Measurements plotted against time form growth curves. The variation in rate of growth with time is the velocity curve. Scammon identified four distinct tissue growth curves:

1. **General Curve (Sigmoid/S-shaped):** Describes overall body size, skeleton, respiratory, and digestive systems. Features rapid growth in infancy, steady growth in childhood, an adolescent growth spurt, and cessation at adulthood.
2. **Neural Curve:** Brain, nervous system, eyes, and skull. Experiences massive early rapid growth. ~95% of total adult size is reached by age 7.
3. **Reproductive Curve:** Primary and secondary sexual characteristics. Remains mostly latent/flat during childhood, followed by extremely rapid growth and maturation during the adolescent spurt (puberty).
4. **Lymphoid Curve:** Thymus, tonsils, spleen, and lymph nodes (immune system). Rapid growth until early adolescence (peaking at ~120% of adult size) and then declines/atrophies due to sex hormones at puberty.

## 3. Methods of Studying Growth

### Longitudinal Method
* **Process:** The same subject or group of subjects is measured repeatedly over a long period (e.g., from birth to maturity).
* **Pros:** Highly accurate for calculating individual rates of growth (velocity) and the timing of specific developmental events (like menarche or peak height velocity).
* **Cons:** Extremely time-consuming, expensive, suffers from sample attrition (subjects dropping out), and requires dedicated record-keeping.

### Cross-Sectional Method
* **Process:** Subjects of different ages are measured at a single point in time (e.g., measuring 100 five-year-olds and 100 ten-year-olds today).
* **Pros:** Fast, cost-effective, and excellent for constructing population standards (like WHO height/weight charts) and calculating centiles.
* **Cons:** Tells us nothing about individual growth rates. It "smooths out" the adolescent growth spurt because early and late maturers are averaged together.

### Mixed-Longitudinal (Sequential) Method
* **Process:** Combines both methods. Multiple age cohorts are followed longitudinally for a short period.
* **Pros:** Overcomes the lengthy time requirement of pure longitudinal studies while still providing rate-of-growth data.

## 4. Stages of Human Growth

The human life cycle is unique among primates for having clearly demarcated phases, particularly childhood and adolescence.

### A. Prenatal Stage (Conception to Birth)
1. **Embryonic Stage (0 - 8 weeks):** Rapid cell division and differentiation. Major organs form (heart beats at 5 weeks). Highly sensitive to teratogens (environmental toxins).
2. **Foetal Stage (9 weeks - Birth):** Massive increase in size and weight. Brain development continues, bones calcify, and lungs mature.

### B. Postnatal Stage
1. **Infancy (Birth to ~3 years):** Characterized by rapid physical and brain growth, reliance on maternal nursing, eruption of deciduous (milk) teeth, and development of basic motor skills (walking).
2. **Childhood (~3 to 10-12 years):** Steady, slow growth. Extensive brain maturation enabling complex language and social learning. Humans have an extended childhood compared to other primates to allow for massive cultural acquisition.
3. **Adolescence (Puberty to ~18/20 years):** Marked by the **Adolescent Growth Spurt (AGS)** and sexual maturation. Girls typically begin AGS 2 years earlier than boys. Peak Height Velocity (PHV) is reached.
4. **Adulthood:** Full reproductive maturity and skeletal maturation (epiphyseal fusion of long bones). A period of physiological homeostasis.
5. **Senescence (Old Age):** Gradual, progressive, and irreversible decline in the structure and function of organs.

## 5. Biological vs. Chronological Age

* **Chronological Age:** The actual time elapsed since birth (e.g., 15 years, 3 months).
* **Biological Age (Physiological Age):** A measure of an individual's developmental or physiological status based on biomarkers. 

Two individuals with a chronological age of 14 may look vastly different because one has reached biological puberty and the other has not. Factors determining biological age include genetics, lifestyle (diet, exercise, stress), and environmental exposures.

## 6. Theories of Ageing (Senescence)

Why do humans age and die? Theories are generally divided into two categories.

### A. Cellular Theories (Intracellular)
* **Hayflick Limit (Telomere Theory):** Normal human cells can only divide 40-60 times. With each division, the protective caps at the ends of chromosomes (telomeres) shorten. When they become too short, the cell enters apoptosis (programmed cell death).
* **Free Radical Theory:** Highly reactive oxygen molecules (free radicals) are produced during normal cellular metabolism. Over time, they cause cumulative damage to DNA, proteins, and lipids, leading to cellular dysfunction. Antioxidants (like Vitamin E) combat this.
* **Error Catastrophe Theory:** Small errors during DNA/RNA replication or protein synthesis accumulate over time. Eventually, these errors cascade into an "error catastrophe," causing the cell to fail.

### B. Extracellular & Evolutionary Theories
* **Collagen/Cross-Linking Theory:** Collagen, a primary structural protein, becomes increasingly cross-linked and rigid with age. This stiffens tissues, impairs cell permeability, and reduces organ efficiency.
* **Immunological Theory:** The immune system's efficacy peaks at puberty and gradually declines (immunosenescence). It becomes less able to fight off pathogens and more prone to auto-immune attacks on the body's own tissues.
* **Disposable Soma Theory (Evolutionary):** Organisms have limited energy resources. Evolution prioritizes allocating energy toward reproductive success rather than indefinite somatic (bodily) maintenance. Once an organism has passed reproductive age, evolutionary selective pressure to maintain the body drops to near zero, leading to senescence.

## 8. Fertility and the Demographic Transition Model

Fertility refers to the actual reproductive performance of a population. A key anthropological and demographic concept is the **Demographic Transition**, which explains the historical shift in birth and death rates.

> **India's Status:** India is currently passing through the late phase of **Stage 3**. The death rate has stabilized at a low level, and the Total Fertility Rate (TFR) has declined below the replacement level of 2.1 in most states.
